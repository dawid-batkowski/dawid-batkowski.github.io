Bot Ops - Project Pitch

"Use resources left in the factory to save yourself from being wiped out by the evil AI. Shoot down and survive the oncoming waves or die."

Bot Ops - Overview

Specifications:
- Engine: TGE (Custom DX11)
- Platform: PC
- My Role: Technical Art, Animation, VFX

This was the fourth group project developed in TGE, inspired by the theme and gameplay of Call of Duty: Black Ops - Zombies. The project focuses heavily on arena-style combat with a wave-based system. The player starts with an MP40 machine gun. By unlocking and exploring new sections of the arena, the player gains access to upgrades and a new weapon - a quadruple-barrel gun capable of damaging multiple enemies in a single shot. After surviving all waves and collecting the final key, the player can escape the arena, completing the game. The game features two difficulty levels, which differ by the presence of checkpoints.

My role focused on VFX and environmental shaders. During the project, I explored a technique called raymarching, which allowed me to create interesting volumetric effects. In addition to environmental effects, a large part of my work was player feedback. I created effects for upgrade machines, muzzle flashes, hit effects, and additional post-processing effects. Lastly, I worked on rigging and animation, including enemy rigs and animations, as well as animated doors.

Featured Work - Volumetric fog & Godrays & Dust particles

Since my first project in this custom engine, I have been exploring ways of creating fog and alternatives to traditional particle systems. Depth- or height-based fog is cheap and often looks good enough; however, for this project, with a first-person perspective, it wasn't sufficient.

My initial attempt at "volumetric" fog involved using a dense cube with many internal intersections and projecting 2D noise in three directions. This produced a somewhat convincing volume-like effect, but due to the high polygon density, it was not scalable. At that point, I continued searching for a better solution and discovered raymarching.

Fog is created by accumulating the density of procedural 3D noise at each step. The raymarching distance is defined by the minimum of a manually set limit and the scene depth. The 3D noise was later extended into fBm using two octaves, adding variation while maintaining low cost.

God rays initially appeared as an unintended by-product of projecting 2D noise in 3D space. After switching to true 3D noise for fog, the god rays disappeared. Based on feedback, I reintroduced god rays as a separate effect using a similar technique—projecting 2D noise along the direction of the directional light direction. To improve realism and prevent them from appearing through ceilings, I incorporated logic to account for shadow mapping so that god rays only appear in lit areas.

Dust particles differ from fog and god rays in that they are not based on density accumulation. Instead, rays march until they intersect small SDF sphere. A single sphere was not sufficient, so I used domain repetition to duplicate the spheres thousands of times with minimal cost. Finally, noise-modulated sine waves were added to create subtle particle movement.

Additional Work - Rigs and Animations

One of my primary responsibilities was creating rigs and animations, mainly for the enemy character and environmental objects.

All rigs and animations were created in Blender.
Enemy animations: movement1, movement2, attack1, attack2, idle, death.
Environmental animations: door open/close, garage door open/close, final gate open.

Additional Work - Shooting Feedback

A significant portion of player feedback relied on VFX. The primary effect for both weapons was a muzzle flash created in EmberGen, baked into flipbooks, and played back in-game. For the shotgun, an additional spark effect was created. This effect was heavily inspired by the SG-225IE Breaker Incendiary shotgun from Helldivers 2. Since we did not yet have a fully developed particle system, I implemented a particle-like effect using a vertex shader (more details here).

Additional shooting feedback included hit effects on enemies, consisting of three parts:
1. Vertex shake - a quick, subtle noise applied to vertex positions of struck enemy on impact to simulate an electrical shock.
2. Electric shock - a pixel shader effect using a panning and modified Minkowski variant of Voronoi noise.
3. Sparks - similar to the shotgun sparks, spawned at the hit location.

Additional Work - Other Effects

1. Bloody screen effect - a fullscreen post-processing shader. A major limitation was the inability to assign textures to the fullscreen pass, so the effect relied heavily on procedural gradient noises. These noises were panned at different speeds and masked at the center to avoid covering the entire screen.

2. Upgrade machine effects - three unique effects, one for each upgrade machine. All were implemented as post-processing effects using noise, sine waves, and UV distortion of scene textures from previous passes. The most complex was the weapon upgrade effect, where the bullet shell, its movement, and its lighting/shading were fully implemented via shader code.

3. Fire propeller - an effect used to justify enemy hovering. The mesh was created in Blender, and vertex painting was used to modify inner, middle, and outer regions of the propeller in the shader separately.
